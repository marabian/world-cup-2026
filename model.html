How this differs from the first report. The earlier page anchored its probabilities to bookmaker lines (DraftKings, Kalshi). This one ignores betting markets entirely: I assigned each of the 24 teams an attack and defence rating from their raw scoring/conceding rates — correcting for schedule inflation (e.g. Belgium's 3.6 goals/game came against UEFA minnows; Curaçao's 0.3 conceded was a CONCACAF artifact) — then simulated the full scoreline distribution. Most picks agree with the market, but the magnitudes differ: my model is notably more skeptical of Uruguay and more draw-leaning on Mexico–Korea.

Methodology — show your work

  1. Raw inputs. For all 24 teams I gathered goals scored/conceded per game and any available expected goals (xG/xGA) over their last ~10–15 competitive matches (2026 qualifiers + 2025–26 friendlies + World Cup Matchday 1), plus strength-of-schedule.
  2. Ratings. Each team gets atk = expected goals vs an average defence, and def = expected goals conceded vs an average attack, on a neutral pitch. Raw rates are corrected: blowout outliers trimmed, minnow-inflated numbers and weak-schedule clean sheets regressed toward World-Cup-level means, and key absences docked (Pulisic, N'Dicka, R. Araújo, Neymar/Rodrygo, Yamal sharpness).
  3. Expected goals per match. λ_home = atk_home × (def_away / 1.35) × venue, and symmetrically for the away side. Host nations (Mexico, USA) get a 1.10 boost, opponent 0.97; all other games are neutral.
  4. Scoreline distribution. A bivariate Poisson grid (0–10 goals each) with the Dixon-Coles low-score correction (ρ = -0.07) to fix the under-counting of 0-0/1-0/0-1/1-1 that pure Poisson suffers from.
  5. Outputs. Win/draw/loss, both-teams-to-score, over/under 2.5 and the most-likely scoreline are all read directly off that probability matrix — my numbers, fully reproducible.
